Abstract

This analysis compares growth trajectories of the S&P 500 index and median household purchasing power (measured via the Median Living Index) across two distinct periods: 2012-2023 (post-crisis recovery) and 2018-2023 (recent inflation period). Using dual baselines to avoid measurement artifacts from the 2008 crisis trough, we document the relative magnitudes of change in financial markets versus household economics. Housing costs emerged as a primary driver of cost increases during the recent period. State-level analysis reveals substantial geographic variation in purchasing power trends.

Observations

Housing costs increased 13.9 percentage points more than goods prices in the 2012-2023 period, reflecting sustained supply constraints in the housing market. However, in the 2018-2023 period, goods inflation (23.6%) slightly exceeded housing inflation (22.4%), with a -1.2 percentage point gap. This reversal reflects the unique dynamics of the COVID-19 pandemic period, where supply chain disruptions drove goods prices higher while remote work reduced housing demand pressures in some markets. Housing comprises the largest share of household expenses and typically shows different inflation dynamics than goods categories, though the recent period represents an exceptional deviation from historical patterns.

Methodology

Data Sources

S&P 500 year-end closing prices from Yahoo Finance. Median household income from US Census Bureau American Community Survey (ACS) Table B19013. Cost of living estimates derived from Bureau of Economic Analysis Regional Price Parities (RPP) adjusted for state-specific consumption patterns using Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey (CEX) weights across 14 major expense categories.

Index Construction

The Median Living Index (MLI) is calculated as the ratio of median household income to estimated annual cost of living for each state. Annual cost of living represents the expenditure required to maintain Bureau of Labor Statistics average consumption patterns at state-specific price levels. Values are inflation-adjusted using the CPI-U to enable temporal comparisons.

Baseline Selection Rationale

Traditional analyses using 2008 as baseline suffer from base-year effects, as 2008 represented the nadir of the Great Recession with S&P 500 at 903.25 and unemployment at 7.3%. Measuring from this trough creates an upward bias in all subsequent metrics. This analysis employs two alternative baselines: 2012 (post-crisis normalization, S&P 500: 1426.19, unemployment: 8.1%) and 2018 (pre-COVID stability, S&P 500: 2506.85, unemployment: 3.9%). The dual baseline approach reveals both long-term trends and recent period dynamics without base-year distortion.

Housing vs Goods Decomposition

Housing costs include rent, mortgage principal/interest, property tax, utilities, and maintenance. Goods categories include food (home and away), apparel, transportation, healthcare, entertainment, personal care, education, and miscellaneous consumption. This decomposition isolates supply-constrained housing inflation from demand-driven goods inflation, with policy relevance for monetary intervention effectiveness.

Important Note on Housing Costs: Housing costs are calculated using BEA Regional Price Parities and BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey weights, which include both renters (facing market rates) and existing homeowners (with fixed mortgage costs). This approach may understate the housing cost burden experienced by renters, recent movers, or new home buyers, who face current market prices. Alternative measures using market rent data (e.g., Zillow ZORI) show higher increases of 30-35% over the 2018-2023 period compared to the 22% reflected in this analysis.
